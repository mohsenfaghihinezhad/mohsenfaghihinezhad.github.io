Description of Competency

This competency focuses on using technology in a purposeful way to support student learning. For me, incorporating technology in teaching does not mean using complicated tools just to make a class look more modern. Instead, technology should help students engage with the material, practice important skills, and receive feedback in ways that support the learning objectives.

In environmental engineering, technology can be useful for visualizing complex systems, checking student understanding, encouraging participation, and helping students connect course material to real environmental problems.

Artifact Rationales

I selected these artifacts because they show my developing approach to using technology as a teaching tool. In the CCT technology session, one important takeaway for me was that even low-key or low-tech tools can make teaching more engaging. Technology does not always need to be advanced. Sometimes a simple polling tool, shared slide, or online activity can help students participate and make their thinking visible.

The Technology Jam activity helped me think about how to match a tool with a learning objective and classroom context. For a large introductory class, I chose slides followed by iClicker as a way to help students distinguish between microcontaminants and emerging contaminants and identify pollutant categories. This type of activity can help students participate without putting too much pressure on individual students, while also giving the instructor a quick way to see whether students are understanding the concept.

The COLA workshop on generative AI also helped me think about technology from a broader perspective. AI is becoming common in higher education, and students may already be using it even if they do not always discuss it openly. Because of that, I think instructors need to create clear expectations and help students build digital literacy rather than assuming they already know how to use these tools responsibly.

Interpretation

This competency helped me realize that technology should be selected based on the learning objective, not based on the tool itself. In the future, before using a technology, I want to ask whether it helps students understand the material, practice a skill, or engage more meaningfully with the course.

I also learned that student comfort with technology matters. One thing I am still thinking about is how to choose tools that are useful but not overwhelming for students, especially when students have different levels of experience with digital platforms. For this reason, I want to use technology in a way that is simple, accessible, and clearly connected to the purpose of the class.

Another important lesson was the need to talk openly about AI. I do not think AI should replace students’ own thinking, but I do think students need guidance on how to use AI ethically and effectively. In my future teaching, I would like to explain when AI use is appropriate, when it is not appropriate, and how students can evaluate AI-generated information critically.

Overall, this competency helped me see technology as a way to support active learning, not as a separate add-on to teaching. My goal is to use technology in ways that make environmental engineering concepts more accessible, interactive, and connected to real-world applications.
