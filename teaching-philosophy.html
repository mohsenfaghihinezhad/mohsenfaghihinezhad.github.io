Teaching Philosophy

Environmental engineering is a broad and interdisciplinary field that connects areas such as water and wastewater treatment, air quality, environmental chemistry, remediation, and sustainability. My teaching philosophy is centered on helping students develop the ability to think like environmental engineers and apply fundamental principles to real-world environmental challenges. Many undergraduate students are only exposed to isolated portions of the discipline and often struggle to understand how different environmental systems interact. One of my primary goals as an instructor is therefore to help students build a broader understanding of the field while learning how to connect concepts across different environmental problems and engineering systems. I want students to recognize that environmental engineering is not only about technical calculations, but also about developing practical solutions that directly affect public health, environmental quality, and communities.

Environmental engineering is also a rapidly evolving discipline that must continuously adapt to emerging environmental and societal challenges. Because of this, I believe it is important to go beyond teaching only conventional and traditional methods. I aim to expose students to both established engineering principles and newer developments in the field, helping them understand not only how systems work, but also where current approaches may be limited and how they can be improved. In my experience, students become more engaged when they see that environmental engineering is an active and innovative discipline that continues to evolve in response to changing environmental needs. Encouraging students to think critically about existing technologies prepares them to adapt to future advancements and contribute to solving complex environmental problems.

At the same time, I believe learning is much more effective when students are genuinely motivated and curious about the material. I intentionally create a sense of “need to know” in the classroom by framing topics around practical environmental challenges and real-world questions. Instead of presenting concepts in isolation, I introduce them through problems that require explanation, such as why certain contaminants persist through treatment systems or why particular technologies fail under specific conditions. I have found that students become far more engaged when they understand the relevance of what they are learning and can connect it to real environmental issues. I also incorporate short interactive elements and occasional light activities to maintain engagement and create a classroom environment where students look forward to participating rather than simply trying to complete the course requirements.

To achieve these goals, I combine structured lectures with active learning and application-based examples. Lectures are used to introduce core concepts, but I regularly incorporate short check-in questions, brief discussions, and case-based examples to keep students engaged and encourage participation. For example, when covering topics such as emerging contaminants or treatment processes, I ask students to evaluate which methods are most appropriate under specific conditions and explain their reasoning. This approach encourages students to move beyond memorization and instead focus on understanding how and why different engineering processes work. I also value collaborative learning because environmental engineering problems are rarely solved from a single perspective. Encouraging students to discuss and approach problems together helps them develop communication skills while learning to evaluate different technical viewpoints.

My assessment methods are designed to reinforce these goals and evaluate students’ ability to apply concepts rather than simply recall information. Homework assignments focus on developing problem-solving skills and strengthening understanding of key engineering principles. In-class exams are used to assess students’ ability to analyze and connect ideas across multiple topics. I also incorporate case study-based activities in which students evaluate realistic environmental problems and justify their decisions. These assessments reflect the type of thinking required in environmental engineering practice, where problems are often interconnected and require integrating knowledge from multiple disciplines. By aligning assessments with classroom activities and learning objectives, I aim to encourage deeper understanding and long-term retention of concepts.

I consider inclusive teaching to be an important part of effective instruction. Students enter environmental engineering courses with different educational backgrounds, experiences, and levels of preparation, which influences how they engage with the material. To address this, I aim to present concepts clearly, use multiple examples, and provide opportunities for students to work together and learn from one another. I also try to incorporate examples from different areas of environmental engineering so students with different interests can connect to the material in meaningful ways. In my experience, students often learn effectively when they are exposed to different perspectives and problem-solving approaches. Creating an environment where students feel comfortable asking questions and participating in discussions is therefore an important part of my teaching philosophy.

Technology is used in my teaching as a tool to improve clarity, organization, and engagement. I use visual materials such as process diagrams, schematics, and treatment system illustrations to explain concepts that are difficult to understand through text alone. I also use digital platforms to organize course materials, communicate with students, and support classroom interaction. These tools are selected based on how effectively they improve student understanding and engagement rather than simply for convenience. As environmental engineering continues to evolve, I also believe it is important for students to become familiar with modern technologies and emerging analytical tools that are increasingly used in engineering practice and research.

Overall, my goal is to create a learning environment that combines strong engineering fundamentals with a broad, practical, and forward-looking perspective. By aligning learning objectives, teaching methods, and assessments, I aim to help students develop the skills, confidence, and critical thinking abilities needed to approach environmental engineering problems in a structured and thoughtful way.
