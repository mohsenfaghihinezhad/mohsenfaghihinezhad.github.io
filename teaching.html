Competency 1: Approved Course in College Teaching

Description of Competency

This competency focuses on developing a foundation in college teaching through completion of an approved graduate-level course. For me, this competency was about learning how to think more intentionally about teaching, not only as explaining material, but as designing a full learning experience for students.

Through this competency, I learned more about writing learning objectives, aligning lectures with activities and assessments, using active learning strategies, and connecting technical concepts to real-world engineering problems.

Course Taken

EGR 811: Foundations of Engineering Education
Michigan State University
Spring 2026

In this course, I completed a course redesign project for ENE 280: Principles of Environmental Engineering and Science, an introductory undergraduate course in environmental engineering. The project helped me think about how a 200-level course can balance fundamental concepts, technical skills, and modern environmental challenges.

Artifacts and Materials Developed

As part of this competency, I developed several teaching materials for ENE 280, including:

  • Redesigned syllabus and 15-week course plan
  • Focus week on emerging contaminants, advanced oxidation processes, and biologically mediated remediation
  • Lecture material for Lecture 28: Emerging Contaminants
  • In-class activity: Selecting Treatment Strategies for Emerging Contaminants
  • Homework assignment and grading rubric on emerging contaminants
  • Final project report and presentation

Artifact Rationales

I selected these artifacts because they show how I applied the ideas from EGR 811 to an actual course design project. The syllabus and course plan show the overall structure of the course and how I tried to balance traditional environmental engineering topics with newer and more applied topics.

One of my main thoughts during the redesign was that environmental engineering is a very broad field. It includes water and wastewater treatment, soil pollution, air pollution, waste management, water resources, and remediation. Because of that, I wanted the course to expose students to the breadth of the field while still keeping the technical foundation.

I also felt that for a 200-level introductory course, some topics should not become too calculation-heavy too early. My goal was not to remove technical rigor, but to make the course more balanced and better connected to why environmental engineering matters.

The in-class activity and homework rubric show my effort to align learning objectives, class activities, and assessment. Students evaluate contaminants, think about their properties, select treatment methods, and justify their choices using engineering reasoning.

Interpretation

EGR 811 changed the way I think about teaching. Before this course, I mostly thought about teaching as explaining the material clearly. I still think clear explanation is very important, but now I see that good teaching also requires careful planning before class starts.

One of the most important things I learned was the importance of alignment. If I want students to apply engineering judgment, then I need to give them activities and assignments where they actually practice that skill. This was one reason I designed the treatment-selection activity and the homework rubric around contaminant properties, treatment limitations, and engineering justification.

Overall, this competency helped me develop a more structured and intentional approach to teaching. It also helped me see the kind of instructor I want to become: someone who teaches technical concepts clearly, connects them to real environmental problems, and creates opportunities for students to actively engage with the material.
