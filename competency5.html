Description of Competency

This competency focuses on assessing student learning in a clear, fair, and meaningful way. For me, assessment is not only about assigning grades. It is also about understanding whether students are reaching the learning objectives, identifying where they may need more support, and using that information to improve teaching.

In environmental engineering, I think assessment should measure both technical understanding and engineering reasoning. Students need to understand definitions and calculations, but they also need to explain why a treatment method works, compare options, and apply concepts to realistic environmental problems.

Artifact Rationales

I selected these artifacts because they show how my understanding of assessment has developed from simply grading student work to thinking about assessment as part of the full learning process. The CCT workshop helped me distinguish between formative assessment, which gives students and instructors feedback during learning, and summative assessment, which evaluates whether learning objectives were achieved.

Bloom’s taxonomy was also useful because it helped me think about the level of learning I am asking students to demonstrate. For example, if I want students to apply engineering judgment, then the assessment should go beyond recalling definitions. It should ask students to analyze, compare, justify, and explain their decisions.

I selected the ENE 280 homework and rubric because they show how I tried to assess different levels of student learning. The homework included conceptual questions, treatment limitations, treatment selection, quantitative analysis, and engineering judgment. This structure allowed students to demonstrate more than memorization.

The grading rubric was especially important because it separated student performance into different levels, such as proficient, developing, and not proficient. This helped make grading more transparent and consistent. It also helped identify common misunderstandings, such as confusing emerging contaminants with conventional pollutants, assuming all contaminants are biodegradable, or selecting treatment methods without considering contaminant properties.

I also selected the in-class treatment-selection activity because it can be used as a quick informal assessment. In this activity, students evaluate contaminants such as PFAS, acetaminophen, microplastics, and TCE, then select treatment methods and justify their choices. By listening to group responses and asking follow-up questions, the instructor can check whether students are connecting contaminant properties to treatment technologies.

Interpretation

This competency helped me think more carefully about the purpose of assessment. Before this work, I mostly thought about assessment as homework, exams, and grading. Now I see assessment as part of the learning process itself.

One of the most important things I learned was the importance of alignment. Learning objectives, class activities, and assessments should support each other. If I want students to apply engineering reasoning, then I need to give them activities and assignments where they actually practice that skill. This was one reason I designed the homework and activity around contaminant properties, treatment limitations, and engineering justification.

I also learned that clear grading policies and rubrics are important for transparency. Students should understand what is being evaluated and why. In my future teaching, I want to use rubrics not just as grading tools, but also as learning guides that help students understand what a strong answer looks like.

Another important takeaway was the role of formative assessment. Short activities, prompt check-ins, group discussion, and follow-up questions can help me understand student learning before a major exam or assignment. This is useful because it gives both the instructor and students a chance to adjust before it is too late.

Overall, this competency helped me see assessment as a way to measure understanding, support student growth, and improve my own teaching. My goal is to design assessments that are fair, transparent, aligned with learning objectives, and connected to real environmental engineering problems.
